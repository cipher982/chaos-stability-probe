What TurboQuant actually is
Quick orientation first, since you'll want to know what you're committing to:
TurboQuant (Zandieh, Daliri, Hadian, Mirrokni — Google Research, ICLR 2026) is an online, training-free, data-oblivious vector quantization algorithm that compresses LLM KV cache to ~3–3.5 bits per value with essentially zero accuracy loss and up to 8× attention speedup on H100s. The core trick is two stages: PolarQuant rotates vectors into polar coordinates so the distribution becomes a concentrated Beta distribution (easy to scalar-quantize per-coordinate), then a 1-bit QJL transform on the residual fixes the bias that MSE-optimal quantizers introduce in inner-product estimation. The paper also proves an information-theoretic lower bound and shows TurboQuant matches it within a ~2.7 constant factor — so this is genuinely "near-optimal" in a provable sense, not just empirically.
That last bit is what makes it special as a paper — it's not just another KV cache method on a long list, it's the one that argues "we're hitting the floor here, further gains require changing the problem."

The core idea you're circling
Lyapunov exponents measure how fast nearby trajectories diverge in a dynamical system. Positive Lyapunov exponent = chaos = exponential sensitivity to initial conditions. The classic intuition: the butterfly flapping its wings, the double pendulum, weather forecasting breaking down past ~10 days no matter how good your model is.
The deep connection — and this is what makes it a genuinely beautiful talk — is that chaos and information theory are the same subject viewed from two angles. Specifically:
Pesin's theorem: for a chaotic system, the Kolmogorov-Sinai entropy (the rate at which the system generates new information) equals the sum of positive Lyapunov exponents. In English: a chaotic system is literally an information source. Each step of the dynamics creates new bits of information that didn't exist before — bits about where exactly the trajectory is, which were hidden in the infinite precision of the initial condition.
This is wild and your team will love it: chaos doesn't destroy information, it creates it. A chaotic system pulls bits up from the microscopic into the macroscopic. Predictability breaks down because the system is generating new information faster than you can measure it.
That's your spine.

Where this connects to AI (without forcing it)
There are several real connections, of varying tightness. I'd pick one or two — not all.
1. Transformers as dynamical systems (tightest, most current)
A forward pass through a deep network is a discrete dynamical system: each layer is a map, the residual stream is a trajectory. People have measured Lyapunov exponents of transformers. There's real work showing that:

Trained networks operate near the "edge of chaos" — neither fully ordered nor fully chaotic
This edge is where information processing is maximized (Langton's old result, rediscovered repeatedly)
Networks that are too ordered can't learn; too chaotic and gradients explode

2. Autoregressive generation as iterated dynamics (most evocative)
When an LLM generates text, it's iterating a map: token → state → next token → state → ... Small perturbations to the prompt or sampling can cascade. Anyone who's tried to reproduce an LLM output across runs knows this. There's a real sense in which long-form generation is a chaotic dynamical system, and "hallucination drift" over long outputs has the flavor of trajectory divergence.
3. Training as a dynamical system (deepest, hardest to present)
SGD is literally a discrete dynamical system on the loss landscape. Edge-of-stability results (Cohen et al. 2021 and follow-ups) show training operates in a regime where the largest Hessian eigenvalue hovers right at the stability boundary — exactly the edge-of-chaos pattern. This is one of the most surprising empirical findings in deep learning theory and almost nobody outside theory circles knows about it.
4. The information-theoretic bridge to your quantization talk (if you wanted to merge)
This is the one where it could connect to your other idea: quantization is about how few bits you need to represent a state; chaos is about how fast a system generates new bits. Both are rate-distortion questions, just on different axes. A chaotic system has an irreducible bit rate that no amount of clever encoding can compress below — that's Pesin. Quantization is about finding the bit floor for a static representation; chaos is about the bit floor for a dynamic one.
That parallel is real and elegant. But honestly — and I'd push back on combining them — I think dynamics-and-chaos deserves its own talk. Trying to bolt it onto quantization risks making both worse.

The honest audit
What's actually true:
Pesin's theorem is real. For a chaotic dynamical system, the Kolmogorov-Sinai entropy equals the sum of positive Lyapunov exponents. That's the bit rate at which the system generates new information, and rate-distortion theory says you can't compress an information source below its entropy rate without loss. What I glossed over:
These two "floors" are floors of different things.
TurboQuant's floor is about compressing a static set of vectors — the KV cache at one moment — under a distortion metric (MSE, inner-product error). It's a spatial / representational bit budget.
Pesin's floor is about how fast a trajectory generates new state information over time. It's a temporal / dynamical bit rate.
Saying "the chaos of the model is the quantization floor" elides this. A chaotic system having entropy rate h doesn't directly tell you the minimum bits per KV vector. It tells you that if you're trying to predict the trajectory, you need h bits of new measurement per unit time to keep up.

The Lyapunov spectrum, mapped onto LLMs
Let me lay out what each regime would look like, as concretely as I can.
Strongly negative Lyapunov (λ << 0): contracting dynamics.
Nearby trajectories converge. Whatever you start with, the system pulls you toward an attractor. In LLM terms: no matter what prompt you give it, the model ends up saying the same thing. This is the degenerate model — mode collapse, repetition loops, "as a large language model I cannot...", the model that always finds its way back to the same fixed point.
Golden Gate Claude is a good example, but it's worth being precise about what converges. Golden Gate Claude doesn't produce identical outputs — it produces topically convergent outputs. Different prompts, different surface text, but the trajectory keeps getting yanked back toward the same semantic basin. That's a contracting dynamics in concept space, not in token space. Token-level it's still varied; concept-level it's collapsed onto an attractor (the bridge).
So you've identified something real: Anthropic literally engineered a contracting dynamical system by clamping a feature. That's a beautiful example for your talk.
Lyapunov near zero: stable, marginal.
Nearby trajectories stay nearby but don't converge. Small differences in input produce small differences in output, indefinitely. This is the boring model — high-quality, predictable, but in the sense that it doesn't surprise you. Two slightly different prompts give two slightly different but parallel outputs forever.
Honestly, I'm not sure this regime really exists for interesting LLMs. Real generative models seem to either contract (collapse) or expand (diverge). Marginal stability is a knife-edge.
Lyapunov positive (λ > 0): chaotic.
Nearby trajectories diverge exponentially. Two prompts that differ by one token end up in completely different places by token 500. This is what most modern LLMs actually do, and you can demonstrate it trivially: take any prompt, change one word, sample at temperature 0.7, watch the outputs walk away from each other.
Lyapunov very large: noise.
Trajectories diverge so fast that the iteration is basically random. The next token bears no useful relationship to the previous state. This is the broken model — untrained, high-temperature, garbage out.

The actual interesting claim
Here's where it clicks, I think. A useful LLM lives in a narrow band of positive Lyapunov. Not zero (boring, contracting toward a fixed point) and not too large (incoherent). Somewhere in the middle.
This is the same "edge of chaos" pattern that shows up everywhere — in cellular automata (Wolfram's class IV), in trained neural networks (the edge of stability result I mentioned earlier), in biological systems. Computation seems to require operating near, but not at, the chaos boundary.
Why? Intuition: a contracting system can't do interesting computation because it forgets its inputs. A fully chaotic system can't do interesting computation because it can't preserve any structure. The interesting regime is the one where information from the input propagates through the dynamics without being either erased or scrambled.
This is what makes LLMs work. The generation process needs to be sensitive enough to inputs that "Tell me a story about cats" produces a different output than "Tell me a story about dogs," but stable enough that the story stays coherent for 1000 tokens.

So what's the connection to compression?
Here's the cleaner version of the chaos-and-quantization link, which I think is what you've been circling:
The bit budget of generation has to match the dynamical regime.
If your model's generation has positive Lyapunov exponent λ, then over T tokens it's "exploring" a space whose effective size grows like e^(λT). To track which trajectory you're on, you need roughly λ·T new bits per generation. Quantize the KV cache too aggressively and you can't preserve the bits needed to stay on the right trajectory — small quantization noise gets amplified by the dynamics into different outputs.
So aggressive quantization works because networks are mostly contractive in most directions, and only fails when it crosses the directions where the dynamics are expanding.
This gives you a real bridge: the quantization floor depends on the dynamical regime the model operates in. A more chaotic model has a higher quantization floor. A more contracting model can be compressed harder.
That's the connection you wanted, I think, and it doesn't require any hand-waving.

What's actually established
The "edge of chaos" hypothesis (Langton, late 1980s, in cellular automata): computation is maximized when a system sits at the phase transition between order and chaos. Not deep in chaos, not deep in order. Right at the boundary.
In Langton's CA experiments, this boundary corresponds to a specific value of a parameter he called λ (different from Lyapunov, confusingly) ≈ 0.5 — roughly the fraction of CA rules that produce non-trivial dynamics. So in that setting, there's a number, and it's near 1/2.
For neural networks at initialization, there's a sharper result. Schoenholz, Pennington, Sohl-Dickstein and others (~2016-2018) showed that deep networks have an "edge of chaos" defined by a specific condition on the weight initialization variance. Initialize with variance too low and signal dies (vanishing gradients). Too high and signal explodes (exploding gradients). The boundary is given by a specific equation involving the activation function. For tanh networks the magic ratio of weight variance is exactly the value where the largest Lyapunov exponent of the forward pass equals zero.
That's a concrete, calculable number. It depends on the activation function. For tanh it's σ²_w ≈ 1.76 / fan_in or thereabouts. He initialization and Xavier initialization are basically engineering rules to put networks at this critical point.
So when you train a network properly, you're already starting it at edge-of-chaos. That's not a coincidence — it's why those initialization schemes work.
For trained networks doing generation, nobody has cleanly measured it. This is the open territory.

Why there probably isn't one magic number for LLMs
The honest reason: LLMs are heterogeneous. Different layers, different attention heads, different token positions all have different dynamical properties. There isn't a single Lyapunov exponent of "the model" — there's a spectrum, and which one matters depends on what you care about.
But here's the suggestive thing: the largest Lyapunov exponent being slightly positive is what you want. Not zero (computation dies), not large (information scrambles). Slightly positive. Where "slightly" probably means something like λ ≈ 1/T_typical where T_typical is the length scale you care about — maybe context length, maybe sentence length, maybe something else.
That's not a universal number. It's a number that depends on what scale you're operating at.

The one place a magic number does show up
There's a genuinely beautiful result that's worth mentioning: the Kelly criterion / log-optimal growth rate in information theory. If you're an information processor that has to balance exploration (chaos) against exploitation (order), the optimal growth rate of information is achieved at a specific point. For binary systems it's at H = 1 bit per step exactly — maximum entropy.
For an LLM, there's a related quantity: the per-token entropy of the output distribution. A model that always picks the top token has zero entropy (fully ordered, contracting). The "magic number" for temperature is famously around 0.7-1.0 for most generation tasks, and nobody fully understands why. It's an empirical sweet spot. The math is correct and the experiments are reasonable, but it's not e.g. DeepMind or FAIR. That's neither a problem nor an endorsement — just context. The QLE framework is genuine and useful regardless of provenance.
The speech-embeddings paper is not about the LLM as a dynamical system — it's about speech as a dynamical system, with the LLM used as an embedding model. Be careful with this. The Lyapunov exponents they compute describe trajectories in semantic space of human-produced speech, embedded by an LLM. They are not measurements of the LLM's own generation dynamics. The paper still gives you the multi-scale finding, but be precise when you cite it: it's evidence that language itself, viewed through LLM embeddings, has the multi-scale dynamical structure.
The Lyapunov Probes paper uses "Lyapunov" loosely. The probes use derivative-based stability constraints — they're inspired by Lyapunov stability theory but not computing Lyapunov exponents directly. That's fine for your talk, but worth being honest about.

The axes, laid out properly
The Li et al. paper distinguishes three dynamical levels, and I want to add a fourth that they don't make explicit but you'll need for your experiment design:
Axis 1: Depth dynamics (intra-network). One forward pass. The "trajectory" is the residual stream as it moves through layers. The "time step" is one layer. This is what Li et al. measured with their 1.32× growth factor — they perturbed the input and watched how the perturbation propagated through 40 layers of Qwen2-14B.
Axis 2: Generation dynamics (iterative / autoregressive). Many forward passes, one per token. The "trajectory" is the sequence of generated tokens, or equivalently the sequence of KV cache states. The "time step" is one token. This is what most people intuitively mean by "LLM chaos" — does the model's output diverge over a long generation?
Axis 3: Reasoning dynamics (chain-of-thought). The trajectory is a sequence of reasoning steps. The "time step" is one reasoning hop. This only applies to CoT-style outputs.
Axis 4 (mine, not Li's): Training dynamics. SGD as a dynamical system on the loss landscape. The "trajectory" is the weight vector evolving over training steps. This is the edge-of-stability / fractal-trainability literature. Different from inference, but worth keeping in mind because your audience will probably ask.
These are genuinely different things. A model could be highly chaotic on Axis 1 (depth) but stable on Axis 2 (generation), or vice versa. Conflating them will make your experimental results uninterpretable.

Which axis your experiment should target
Your instinct — perturb the input, see what happens to the output — is Axis 2 (generation dynamics). You're asking: "if I change the prompt slightly, how fast do the outputs diverge?"
This is a great choice for a learning club talk because:

It's the most intuitive axis for an AI eng audience. Everyone has stared at unexpectedly different LLM outputs from similar prompts. You're putting a number on a phenomenon they already feel.
It's measurable with API calls only. No mechanistic interp, no logit access, no internal probing. Just text in, text out.
It directly addresses "do different models diverge differently?" — your actual question.
It's not what Li et al. measured. Their experiments were on Axis 1 (depth, single forward pass). You'd be doing complementary work, not duplicating.

But — and this is important — Axis 2 is harder to define rigorously than Axis 1. On Axis 1 you have a continuous representation space, a clear notion of distance (cosine similarity, L2), and a clean perturbation (add ε to the input embedding). On Axis 2 your "state" is a sequence of discrete tokens, and you need to define what "distance between trajectories" means.
This is the design question to think hardest about.

Experiment design
Here's a concrete proposal. Resist the urge to over-engineer the first version — get something running, eyeball the results, then decide what's worth measuring properly.
Step 1: Define your perturbation.
Options, ordered from cleanest to messiest:

Synonym swap. Replace one word with a near-synonym ("happy" → "joyful"). Smallest semantic perturbation.
Token swap. Replace one token with a similar token (closest in embedding space). Cleanest but may produce ungrammatical inputs.
Punctuation/whitespace. Add a comma, change capitalization. The perturbation closest to "infinitesimal" — semantically zero, lexically nonzero.
Paraphrase. Two semantically-equivalent rewordings. Largest perturbation, but most natural.

I'd start with option 1 (synonym swap) because it's a clear, controlled, repeatable perturbation that's still recognizably "the same prompt" to a human. Option 3 is interesting as a control — if the model's outputs diverge under whitespace changes, that's strong evidence of chaos rather than meaningful response to input.
Step 2: Define your divergence metric.
You need to measure how far apart two outputs are. Options:

Token-level edit distance over time. Compute Levenshtein distance between outputs at each token position. Plot it. Easy, but punishes synonyms.
Embedding distance between outputs. Embed both full outputs with a sentence transformer, take cosine distance. Captures semantic divergence, ignores surface form.
Topic / structure divergence. Use an LLM-as-judge to score "are these outputs about the same thing." Most semantic, least reproducible.
Per-token KL divergence on logprobs. If you have logprob access, compute the KL between the two output distributions at each token position. This is the cleanest measurement but requires logprob access.

For a first pass: do both (1) and (2). They measure different things. Edit distance shows when outputs go literally different. Embedding distance shows when they go semantically different. Plotting both on the same chart is informative.
Step 3: Define your protocol.
For each (model, prompt, perturbation) triple:

Sample N completions of length T from the unperturbed prompt at temperature 0 (deterministic) — call this the reference.
Sample N completions of length T from the perturbed prompt at temperature 0.
At each token position t ∈ [1..T], compute divergence between reference and perturbed.
Plot divergence vs. t.

If divergence grows roughly exponentially in t, you have a Lyapunov-like signature, and the slope is your λ-analog.
Critical design choice: temperature = 0. If you sample at temperature > 0, you can't distinguish chaos (sensitivity to input) from stochasticity (sensitivity to random seed). At temperature 0, any divergence between the two outputs is causally attributable to the input perturbation. This is the version of the experiment that has a clean interpretation.
(Caveat: at temp 0, identical inputs sometimes still diverge across hardware / batch size due to floating-point non-associativity. You can either ignore this — it's small — or run a "control": same prompt twice, see how much the outputs differ. That's your noise floor.)
Step 4: Models to compare.
Pick a span:

A small model (e.g., Llama-3.2-1B or 3B)
A mid model (Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen2.5-7B)
A large model (Llama-3.1-70B, Qwen2.5-72B)
An RLHF'd / instruction-tuned model (Claude, GPT-4)
A base model (any of the above pre-RLHF, if available)

The interesting hypotheses to look for:

Smaller models diverge faster. Less capacity = more sensitivity to input noise.
RLHF'd models diverge slower. RLHF pulls models toward attractors (politeness, certain phrasings) — that's contracting dynamics.
Reasoning-tuned models (o1, R1) diverge differently than base chat models. The reasoning step might either amplify or damp perturbations.

I'd be especially curious about #2. If true, it's a real, presentable finding: RLHF measurably reduces dynamical chaos in LLM generation. That's a tweet-worthy result and exactly the kind of thing your team would want to know.

Things that will trip up the experiment
A few things worth knowing in advance so you don't get confused by the data:
Hardware non-determinism. Even at temp 0, different GPUs / batch sizes / inference frameworks will produce slightly different outputs. Run your reference and perturbed completions on identical hardware, ideally in the same batch.
API-served models hide non-determinism. Claude/GPT/Gemini through APIs are not deterministic at temp 0 because of batching effects on the server side. If you use APIs, run the same prompt many times and treat the variance as a noise floor. Or use a local model where you can pin everything.
Token-level metrics are noisy near the start. The first few tokens often look identical because the model hasn't "decided" to go a different direction yet. Don't panic if divergence is flat for the first 5-10 tokens then takes off — that's expected. The interesting question is the exponential rate once divergence starts.
Prompt sensitivity is highly task-dependent. A creative writing prompt will diverge much faster than a math problem (which has a single correct answer the model is converging toward). Stratify your experiments by task type: factual QA, creative generation, code, reasoning. Each will have a different λ.
Embedding distance saturates. Once two outputs are completely different, cosine distance plateaus near 1. So if you run T = 500 tokens, you'll see divergence climb and then flatten. The slope of the rising portion is what matters, not the saturation level.

What you might actually find (predictions worth charting against)
Predictions to commit to before you run the experiment, so you can be surprised:

Divergence will grow approximately linearly in log-space, i.e., exponentially in token position, for the rising portion. Slope = effective Lyapunov exponent.
RLHF'd / instruction-tuned models will have lower λ than base models.
Larger models within the same family will have lower λ than smaller models.
Math/code prompts will have lower λ than creative prompts.
Reasoning models (o1, R1) on math will have very low λ (they're converging to a fixed point — the answer). On creative tasks the comparison is murkier.

If most of these come out the way I'd guess, you have a clean talk-worthy result. If some flip, that's more interesting and even better for the talk.
